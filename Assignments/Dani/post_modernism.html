PostModernism is a movement or in other words - a method of philosophical thoughts that gained widespread acceptance in the late 1970s and was born in response to modernism. Definition

People usually get very confused with terminology postmodernism and what it actually is and very often mix it up with modernism. But in true words, no one has ever identified it in a perfect way.Postmodernism does not replace modernism, both of them coexist. The style does not reject everything,it questions everything.Postmodernism is a very general and wide-ranging term which is applied to literature, art, philosophy, architecture, fiction, and cultural and literary criticism, among others. Postmodernism is largely a reaction to the assumptions of scientific, or objective efforts to explain reality. It stems from a recognition that reality is not simply mirrored in human understanding of it, but rather, is constructed as the mind tries to understand its own particular and personal reality. Associated with “anything goes” pluralism, fragmentation, allusions, allegory and quotations, postmodernism will remain vague, since those who claim to be postmodernists have varying beliefs and opinions on issues.

Characteristics

When listing the characteristics of postmodernism, it is important to remember that postmodernists do not place their philosophy in a defined box or category. As mentioned before ,their beliefs and practices are personal rather than being identifiable with a particular establishment or special interest group. There are many characteristics of postmodernism, such as:

  1. Self-conceptualization and rationalization
  2. Truth and error are synonymous
  3. Ownership
  4. Morality is personal
  5. Morality is personal
  6. Globalization
  7. Liberal ethics
  8. Disillusionment with modernism
  9. All religions are valid

Roy Lichtenstein

Roy Lichtenstein was one of the first American Pop artists to achieve widespread renown, and he became a lightning rod for criticism of the movement. His early work ranged widely in style and subject matter, and displayed considerable understanding of postmodernist painting: Lichtenstein would often maintain that he was as interested in the abstract qualities of his images as he was in their subject matter.
